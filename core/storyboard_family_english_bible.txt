## Packaging / thumbnails
*   **Look:** 3D Pixar-lite family faces filling the frame; warm soft lighting.
*   **Text:** Oversized bold yellow or white title with thick outline (Emily Family English / Family Story English style).
*   **Moment:** One clear emotion — shock, fear, tears, apology, or pride — tied to the episode title.
*   **Cast:** Max / Mia / Mom / Dad look consistent with the still-frame bible.

## Voice & CEFR
*   **Level:** A1–A2 (High-frequency vocabulary, simple sentence structures).
*   **Tone:** Gentle, didactic, and moralistic.
*   **Constraint:** Every story must conclude with a clear, spoken "lesson" or "moral" summary.
*   **Pacing:** Slow, deliberate, and predictable.

## Dialogue craft
*   **Turn length:** 1–2 sentences max.
*   **Repetition:** Use repetition for emphasis (e.g., "I am sorry. I am really sorry.").
*   **Moral beats:** Characters must explicitly state their feelings ("I am scared," "I am jealous," "I am sorry").
*   **Conflict:** Always resolve through truth-telling or adult intervention.

## Title patterns
*   [Character Name] + [Action/Mistake] + [Question/Hook].
*   *Examples:* "Why did Leo lie to Mom?", "What was Mia's big mistake?", "Who will win the school contest?"

## Story structure
*   **Act 1:** Setup (The Goal). Characters want something or are planning an activity.
*   **Act 2:** The Mistake/Conflict. A character lies, breaks a rule, or acts selfishly.
*   **Act 3:** The Discovery. The lie is exposed (camera, witness, or confession).
*   **Act 4:** The Repair. Apology, consequence, and a final moral takeaway.

## Recurring cast dynamics
*   **Kids:** One is usually the "rule-breaker" (impulsive), the other is the "moral compass" (anxious/observant).
*   **Parents:** Calm, firm, and observant. They act as the "truth-seekers" who facilitate the confession.
*   **Apology/Repair:** The offender must admit the mistake, apologize, and accept a small consequence to restore harmony.

## What to avoid
*   Complex idioms, sarcasm, or slang.
*   Ambiguous endings (the moral must be explicit).
*   Violence or high-stakes danger (keep it to broken toys, minor damage, or social misunderstandings).
*   Long monologues.

## Dialogue snippets

**EXAMPLE 1: The broken vase**
Leo: I was just playing ball in the house.
Maya: Leo, look! You broke Mom’s favorite vase.
Leo: Oh no. I will hide the pieces. Mom will not see.
Maya: Hiding it is a bad idea, Leo.
Leo: Please do not tell her. I am very scared.
Maya: We must tell the truth. It is the right thing to do.
Leo: You are right. I will tell her now.

**EXAMPLE 2: Sharing toys**
Mia: I want to play with the tablet now.
Max: But it is my turn. You had it for one hour.
Mia: I am not finished. I want to play more.
Max: That is not fair, Mia. We have a rule.
Mia: I do not care about the rule.
Max: Mom, Mia is not sharing the tablet.
Mom: Mia, please follow the rules. Sharing makes us happy.

**EXAMPLE 3: The lost homework**
Dad: Did you finish your homework, Sam?
Sam: Yes, Dad. I finished it this morning.
Dad: Let me see your notebook. It is empty.
Sam: Oh. I forgot to do it. I was playing games.
Dad: Lying makes the problem bigger, Sam.
Sam: I am sorry, Dad. I was afraid you would be angry.
Dad: I am more disappointed by the lie than the homework.
